Depression vs. Covid Fatigue: Data Science Capstone

Apr 29, 29294 | 3 minutes read
Share this: Twitter Facebook Pinterest

Tags: capstone, twitter data, topic modeling

Motivation

Since the onset of the pandemic, depression has risen, but so has covid fatigue and blues (general feeling of tiredness and sadness). Although the line between blues and depression can be blurry at times, the most obvious difference is in the length of their symptoms. Depressed feelings last much longer with occasional moments of joy, whereas blues can come and go in shorter episodes. Nonetheless, people affected by either pandemic blues or depression have found substantial support groups (online communities around a hashtag) on Twitter to voice their experiences and reassure others going through similar symptoms. This is especially true now given that the pandemic, remote schooling, work from home, etc are all stressors that the entire world is experiencing at once.

Pandemic-specific groups such as #covidfatigue, #covidblues and #pandemicblues were formed during the pandemic, while groups such as #livingwithdepression and #depression had already existed before. The formation of these pandemic-specific groups provides a natural comparison to pre-pandemic groups, regarding the use of language and rhetoric used by respective members in each group to cope and heal from their symptoms. This comparison of language and rhetoric can help bring forth specific symptoms that may be more prevalent in each group. It can also help doctors understand their clients and their needs better.

The end goal is to have a working model that is able to distinguish tweets between the two groups with acceptable accuracy. But ultimately, insight such as what phrases, rhetoric, etc. are used by each group to cope with their symptoms can be more valuable when it comes to the question of what is the proper treatment for people suffering from the blues, depression, or both.

Data Description

The data set consists of tweets pulled from Twitter using two predefined groups of hashtags during the month of March 2021. #depression and #livingwithdepression made up the depression group. #covidfatigue, #pandemicblues, #covidburnout, #pandemicfatigue and #covidblues made up the second group. Unlike covid hashtags, depression hashtags have an extensive and established history on Twitter. As such, there were significantly more Twitter users posting and re-posting tweets under depression hashtags than covid fatigue hashtags. In total, 12,845 tweets were extracted from depression hashtags; of which a random sample of 1,626 were drawn to be included into the data set. 543 tweets were extracted from covid fatigue hashtags. Collectively, the data set contained 2,169 tweets, of which 80% is used for training and 20% for testing.

Methodology

  • Extract Tweets for the two class of hashtags
  • Clean and preprocess tweets
  • Extract topics using LDA topic modeling
  • Assign probabilities to tweets and train
  • Classify new tweets
